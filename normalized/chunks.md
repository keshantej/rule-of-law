Arizona's Rule of Law Day: Why It Matters, and Why It Starts With Us
“If we don't protect the Rule of Law, we cannot expect the law to protect us.”
On February 12, 2026, something quietly profound will happen in courthouses across
Arizona. From Flagstaff to Yuma, lawyers and judges will gather not to argue cases or issue
rulings, but to reaffirm a shared civic commitment: the Rule of Law. In Tucson, the State
Bar of Arizona and the Arizona Supreme Court will host a special ceremony at the Pima
County Superior Court from 4:00 to 5:00 p.m., featuring Chief Justice Ann Timmer of the
Arizona Supreme Court, Presiding Superior Court Judge Danelle Liwski, and University of
Arizona James E. Rogers College of Law Dean Jason Kreag.
The date itself is rich with meaning. February 12 marks Arizona's Rule of Law Day. It also
coincides with the 250th birthday of the Declaration of Independence and the birthday of
Abraham Lincoln-a president who understood, perhaps better than any other, that
democracy survives only when laws, not passions or power, rule the day.
The phrase “Rule of Law” is often invoked, sometimes casually, but its meaning is neither
vague nor abstract. The World Justice Project defines the Rule of Law through four core
principles: accountability (that government officials and private actors alike are subject to
the law); just law (that laws are clear, publicized, stable, and protect fundamental rights);
open government (that laws are enacted and enforced transparently); and accessible,
impartial justice (that justice is delivered by competent, ethical, and independent decision-
makers). In short, no one is above the law, the rules are known in advance, and disputes
are resolved fairly.
It is vitally important because if we don't protect the Rule of Law, we cannot expect the
law to protect us.
This framework is not a luxury of stable societies; it is the reason they remain stable. The
Rule of Law is what transforms disagreement into dialogue, conflict into process, and
power into responsibility. Without it, rights become aspirations rather than guarantees, and
democracy becomes a slogan rather than a system.

Lawyers and judges occupy a unique position in this ecosystem. Lawyers swear an oath
not to clients alone, but to the Constitution and the laws of Arizona and the United States.
Judges, in turn, are entrusted to apply those laws impartially, without fear or favor. This
shared professional obligation makes lawyers especially well-suited to promote, defend,
and protect the Rule of Law-not as a partisan cause, but as a civic one.
That is why, during the February 12 ceremony, lawyers across the state will retake their
oath. It is a symbolic act, but symbols matter. They remind us that the Rule of Law is not
self-executing. It depends on people-judges who decide cases with integrity, lawyers who
advocate ethically, and citizens who respect lawful outcomes even when they disagree.
Arizona's Rule of Law Day is not only for lawyers. It is an invitation to all Arizonans to
recommit to the idea that our democracy works best when we insist on fairness,
accountability, and equal justice. Under the leadership of our courts and our legal
profession-and with the engagement of an informed and principled public-we can
ensure that the Rule of Law remains not just an ideal we celebrate once a year, but a
practice we defend every day.
Ted Schmidt is managing partner of the Tucson law firm Schmidt, Sethi & Akmajian and
immediate past president of the State Bar of Arizona

President's Message-The Rule of Law is Under Attack-Why We Should Care?
Ted Schmidt
In 2006 the American Bar Association and 21 partners created the World Justice Project - an
“international civil society organization working to advance the rule of law around the world". The
WJP Rule of Law Index® evaluates 142 countries and jurisdictions around the world adhere to the
rule of law, which it defines as a durable system of laws, institutions, norms, and community
commitment that delivers four universal principals: accountability, just law, open government, and
accessible and impartial justice. For the sixth year in a row, the rule of law has declined in most
countries. The United States ranks 26th out of 142 world countries. The top 4 are the Scandinavian
countries. https://worldjusticeproject.org/rule-of-law-index/global/2023
China's suppression of political dissent and human rights abuses, Venezuela's President Maduro's
disqualification of opposition candidates and manipulation of the ballot box, and Russia's
assassination of dissidents are just a handful of examples of the demise of the rule of law around
the world. Wherever autocracies exist the rule of law suffers. This is not surprising. “Power tends to
corrupt and absolute power corrupts absolutely” Lord Acton (1887).
It is undeniable that the Rule of Law is under attack around the world and, yes, right here in the
good ol' USA. Albeit for different reasons, Republicans, Democrats, liberals, conservatives and
moderates all seem to agree, the Rule of Law is taking hits.
So why should we care and what can we do about it?
As lawyers we are in the best position to advocate and work towards the preservation of the rule of
law. Members of our communities look to us to inform, educate and advise regarding all aspects of
the law.
Clearly the underlying principles of fairness, equality, and justice, and their importance to our legal
system must be the cornerstones of our profession. In our oath we swear to “uphold the U.S. and
Arizona Constitutions.” I submit that as lawyers we in fact have a moral obligation to speak up, to
educate, and lead our communities in fighting off these attacks and strengthening this hallowed
principle.
Even those who might choose to take a cynical view on the subject must admit that their very
livelihood is at stake here. Without the rule of law, how do we advise our clients what to do and not
do, how do we predict outcomes and how do we effectively represent them if we cannot depend on
the sanctity, consistency, and fairness of the law?
When Hitler invaded Europe, and Stalin and Mao Zedong overthrew the governments in Russia and
China, the first in those societies to come under attack and lose relevancy were those most able to
protect citizens from human rights abuses, genocide, and political persecution-lawyers and
judges.
“The only thing necessary for the triumph of evil is for good men to do nothing.” Edmund Burke.

President's Message-Defining the Rule of Law
If we as lawyers and leaders in our communities are to defend and advance the rule of law it is
important as a first step that we fully understand what this evolving principle central to our
democracy truly means.
As early as 400 BC it was recognized in Ancient Greece that the rule of law was a cornerstone to a
functioning democracy. Aristotle put it simply, "It is more proper that law should govern than any
one of the citizens: upon the same principle, if it is advantageous to place the supreme power in
some particular persons, they should be appointed to be only guardians, and the servants of the
laws."
In the 1600s John Locke, an English philosopher who played a key role in the development of
western democracy emphasized the importance of the rule of law to that institution. "Wherever
Law ends, tyranny begins."
Our forefathers relied heavily upon this principal both in the Declaration of Independence and
Constitution. Thomas Jefferson said, "The most sacred of the duties of a government [is] to do
equal and impartial justice to all its citizens."
And of course, when the rule of law met its most stringent test in America, President Abraham
Lincoln exclaimed, ""Let every American, every lover of liberty, every well-wisher to his posterity,
swear by the blood of the Revolution never to violate in the least particular the laws of the country,
and never to tolerate their violation by others."
In its most basic form, the rule of law is understood to mean that we are governed by laws, not the
whims of our leaders and that those laws are fair, just and applied equally to all. That said, the
concept is a dynamic and evolving standard

On April 26, 2024, the International Academy of Trial Lawyers published what I believe to be the
most comprehensive definition of the rule of law. This definition I believe is one we should all be
familiar with and use as a guide as we repel attacks on this foundation of our democracy and
profession.
The International Academy of Trial Lawyers steadfastly advocates for, and defends, the Rule
of Law. In doing so, we refer to the principle that all persons, institutions, and entities
(including corporations and governments in any form):
• are subject to law;
• have access to the protections of and rights established through the law regardless of
economic means; and
• may have their or its actions challenged in, and may be held accountable before, a court
of law.
This system requires that the legal profession operates independently and without
interference from others, ensuring the greatest possible access to legal protections and
remedies.
Further, we hold that to conform to the Rule of Law, laws must be:
• clearly defined, easily accessible and legally certain;
• established through a transparent process that is open to debate and discussion;
• prospective in nature;
• consistent with internationally recognized human rights principles;
• enforceable through a court of law where disputes are timely adjudicated by an
independent and impartial judiciary, and may include decision-making by a group of
impartial citizens, on the basis of evidence applied to established law, free from case-
related bias or ethical conflicts; and
• impartially enforced by the state.
I hope you, as I, will embrace this definition and help bring members in your community and
practice to also understand it and appreciate its importance. The rule of law is under attack in our
country and as such our very democracy is at risk. In my January message I will posit some ideas as
to how we bring the temperature down, bring civility back into the room and promote respect and
understanding among divergent views. If we can do that, I believe the rule of law will indeed endure.
Finally, I recommend to you the World Justice Project, an effort to promote the rule of law
throughout the world. Here you will find a wealth of information regarding how the rule of law is
fairing around the world and what we can do as vanguards to continue its advancement.

You Can Hear but Did you Listen? -Facilitating Dialogue
Has there ever been a more divisive time in our society since the Civil War? We have so much to be
divisive about. Democrat-Republican, climate change, abortion, gun rights, BLM, police reform,
gender identity. . . Where to start? And these divides are only driven deeper by social media and
news sources focused to political leanings.
If we as lawyers are going to fulfill our oath of office and protect the Constitution, we need to know
how to face this divisiveness and lead constructive, meaningful dialogue among those of different
points of view.
There has been much studied and written on this subject. I commend “Beyond Your Bubble-How
to Connect Across the Political Divide,” by Tania Israel, and Time Magazine “9 Ways to Respond to
Political Information” by Angela Haupt (October 9,2004). Tania is a professor at the University of
California at Santa Barbara with a doctorate in counseling. This is her specialty. Angela is a health
and wellness editor who interviewed a number of experts for her piece.
As a starting point, Israel tell us one of the best ways to turning down the temperature, while
bringing people of different points of view together especially at a meeting or event is to establish
ground rules. Begin the discussion by asking all to treat the discussion as a dialogue, a civil and
open discussion where we hear to each other, honestly listen and understand what is being said,
while avoiding a judgmental response.
Approach the dialogue with the understanding it is not our goal to convince those who disagree to
adopt our point of view. We are not in dialogue to persuade. We are in dialogue to truly listen,
understand and respect each other's point of view. If we can understand why and where we are
each coming from, maybe different points of view make more sense. Maybe we even find some
common ground.
The main point, is we approach the dialogue with the mindset that we are not engaging in this
conversation to “win or lose.” Rather we each want to truly listen and understand. Not to be thinking
how to respond while listening but keeping the mind open to just listening and understanding. Help
each person in the dialogue to feel safe and understood. Not judged. Do not argue or confront.
Manage emotions always bringing your focus back to the fact you are the lawyer, the facilitator, the
person leading this dialogue.

SLIDE 4 FOUR CORE PRINCIPLES
1.5 - 2 minutes
⏱
KEY MESSAGE Give the audience a framework they can carry with them - four principles, clearly
named.
The World Justice Project - an international organization that tracks the Rule of Law in 142
countries - breaks this concept into four core principles. These are the building blocks.
First: Accountability. Government officials and private actors alike are subject to the law. No
one receives a pass simply because of rank, wealth, or political affiliation.
Second: Just Law. Laws must be clear, publicly available, stable over time, and protective of
fundamental rights. You have to be able to know the rules before you are held to them.
Third: Open Government. Laws should be enacted and enforced transparently. The process
matters as much as the outcome - and a government that operates in the shadows is a
government that has stopped answering to the people.
Fourth: Accessible and Impartial Justice. Justice must be delivered by competent,
independent decision-makers - and it must be accessible to everyone, not just to those
who can afford it.
When all four of these principles are functioning together, people can trust that outcomes
will be fair - even when they disagree with the result. That trust is the foundation everything
else is built on.

SLIDE 12 HANDLING DISAGREEMENT AND MISINFORMATION

KEY MESSAGE Give ambassadors practical tools - not theory - for staying productive when a

You will encounter disagreement. You will hear misinformation. You may hear things that
are factually wrong and that frustrate you. Here is how to handle it without losing the room.
Ask where the information came from. Not confrontationally - with genuine curiosity.
'Where did you hear that?' opens a door. It lets you understand the source of the concern
and creates space to examine how different outlets frame the same facts differently.
Identify the underlying concern. People often repeat misinformation because it speaks to
a real fear. Ask what concerns them most about what they've heard. If you can address
the fear rather than just correct the fact, you're having a more productive conversation.
Stay calm. You are the lawyer in the room. People will look to you to model the kind of
reasoned, civil discourse you are asking of them. If you become combative, the
conversation ends - and you've undermined everything you came to say.
Remember your goal. You are not there to change anyone's mind in a single presentation.

Four Core Principles — World Justice Project
I

Accountability

No person or institution is above the law — government officials and private actors alike remain subject to legal authority.

II

Just Law

Laws must be clear, publicized, stable, and protective of fundamental rights — not merely rules on paper.

III

Open Government

Legal rules and decisions are enacted and enforced transparently, visible to citizens and accountable to public review.

IV

Accessible, Impartial Justice

Disputes are resolved fairly by competent, ethical, and independent decision-makers available to ordinary people.

Handling Difficult Questions

When it turns political

Return the discussion to constitutional process, fairness, equal application of law, and the institutions that allow disagreement to remain peaceful. Distinguish principle from policy preference.

"Is the Rule of Law just any law?"

No. Rule by law means any rule is enforced. The Rule of Law requires that laws themselves meet standards of fairness, transparency, stability, and rights protection.

"Why should lawyers speak on this?"

The professional oath binds attorneys to the Constitution and legal system itself — not only to clients. Lawyers are civic translators between legal principle and public understanding.

"How is this different from majority rule?"

Majority rule determines who wins elections. The Rule of Law constrains what winners can do — protecting minority rights and ensuring power is exercised through rules, not personal will.
